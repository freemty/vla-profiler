DiT Per-Step Cost vs Params

Log-log scatter showing how DiT action head cost scales with parameter count. The slope change at 174M–350M marks the compute-bound to memory-bandwidth-bound transition.

Transition zone (174M–350M)
Key insight: Below ~174M params the DiT is compute-bound — latency scales sub-linearly because GPU compute cores are the bottleneck and arithmetic intensity is high. Above ~350M params it becomes memory-bandwidth-bound — weight loading dominates and latency grows faster. NitroGen (174M) sits at the sweet spot just before the transition, achieving 55.9 Hz at k=1. LingBot-VA's 5B WanDiT (28.5ms/step) is surprisingly fast per-step due to grouped-query attention reducing memory traffic.

NitroGen k-Sweep — Denoising Steps

Total latency and control frequency as a function of denoising steps k. Perfect linearity confirms DiT compute cost is the sole bottleneck — no caching, no quadratic terms.

NitroGen total latency vs denoising steps k (dual y-axis)
k = 1,2,4,8,16 · RTX 5880 Ada · reference lines: 10Hz (robot) and 30Hz (real-time)

Frequency (Hz) — right axis
30 Hz (real-time threshold)
10 Hz (robot control threshold)
Key insight: Latency scales perfectly linearly with k (R² ≈ 1.0). The non-zero intercept (~10ms) corresponds to encode+context overhead, which is amortized across steps. At k=1 (55.9 Hz) NitroGen exceeds the 30 Hz real-time threshold. At k=4 (25.4 Hz) it still beats the 10 Hz robot control threshold. k=16 (7.9 Hz) pushes into WAM territory while retaining diffusion quality.

Key insight: The paradigm choice is a hard latency-generalization tradeoff. ACT gives the lowest latency but cannot generalize beyond its training distribution. WAM (full) offers explicit world-model planning but is 700x slower than ACT.
